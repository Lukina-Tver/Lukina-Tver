# The Demon: From Lermontov's Poetry to Vrubel's Vision

> *"A lion's nature! A terrifying and mighty spirit! 'The Demon' has become a fact of my life..."* — V. Belinskiy

---

## I. The Fatal Turn and Heavenly Mercy
In the final part of the poem, a fatal turn occurs in the Demon's love—now he fights for Tamara against God Himself. Yet, a noble soul capable of true love deserves the immortality of the heavens.

### The Duel for the Soul
> "No more your sanctuary is here,  
> Here I possess, and I hold dear."

When the Angel explains God’s mercy at the private Judgment, we see the redemption of Tamara through her suffering and love:
> "She suffered and she loved—it seemed  
> That paradise for love has beamed!.."

---

## II. The Heritage of the Spirit: Vrubel’s Demon
There is a mystical connection between the poet and the artist. Mikhail Vrubel was born in 1856—the same year Lermontov’s poem was first published in Germany. Both were haunted by this "mighty image."

In Vrubel’s masterpieces, we see the Demon not just as a fallen angel, but as a proud thinker and a suffering rebel against the world's injustice. He stands alone against the Cosmos, depicted against the backdrop of the fathomless expanse of the Universe.

---

## III. Philosophical Roots
The plot is based on ancient biblical legends of Lucifer and oriental motifs from the Quran:
> "And when We said to the angels: 'Prostrate yourselves before Adam,' they all prostrated themselves, except Iblis, who was created from fire." (The Quran)

Lermontov also drew upon the folklore of the Caucasian peoples, blending universal myths with the rugged beauty of the mountains.

---

## IV. The Poet’s Parting with the Dream
By the late 1830s, Lermontov began to distance himself from his creation. In his later work, "A Fairy Tale for Children," he looked back at the Demon as a "childish delirium" that he finally escaped through his verses.
